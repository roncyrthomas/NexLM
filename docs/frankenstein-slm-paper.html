Tri-Memory Neuromodulation

Unifying Symbolic Graph Retrieval, Test-Time Neural Memory, and Hebbian Skill Plasticity in a 700M Hybrid SSM via Affect-Gated Learning Rates

Vaisakh Constantine  ·  NexAnalytica Research
Design Report · 2026-05-28

Abstract

We present the design of a 700M-parameter small language model that integrates seven architectural advances developed independently across the 2024–2026 literature: (i) the subquadratic state-space layers of Mamba2 1; (ii) the noise-cancelling differential attention of the DIFF Transformer 2; (iii) the Samba-style hybrid SSM–attention interleaving pattern 3; (iv) the test-time-trained neural memory module of Titans in its Memory-as-Gating configuration 4; (v) the Personalized-PageRank graph retrieval of HippoRAG 5; (vi) Granite-style structured tool calling 6; and (vii) a neuromodulatory affect signal — inspired by HormoneT5 7 and reward-gated Hebbian plasticity 8 — that uniformly governs writes across three independent memory timescales. Our central thesis is not the introduction of any single architectural block, but rather the demonstration that three independent memory systems operating at three distinct timescales (symbolic graph, parametric neural, and Hebbian fast-weight) can be coherently unified by a single affect-driven scheduler. We argue this is the minimal architectural commitment needed to produce a small model that can learn during deployment without catastrophic collapse. This report functions as both a literature review of the constituent works and a complete design specification for the model.

Introduction and Motivation

The past three years have produced a remarkable proliferation of architectural advances for language models, but these advances have largely been published in isolation. State-space models have reduced inference cost. Test-time-trained memory modules have offered a route to long-term recall without unbounded context. Knowledge-graph retrieval has improved multi-hop reasoning. Neuromodulatory inspired-by-biology controllers have appeared in early prototypes. Each of these strands matures in its own corner of the literature, evaluated against its own baselines, with little cross-pollination.

This report proposes a deliberately maximalist synthesis. Rather than refining any single piece, we combine seven distinct advances into a single coherent architecture, with the explicit goal of producing a small model (700M parameters) that can learn during use — not merely retrieve from a static index, not merely cache recent context, but actually adapt its weights in response to interaction outcomes, while remaining stable enough to deploy as a personal agent.

The motivating use case is what we call the persistent analyst: a small model deployed on local hardware that ingests a user's documents into a structured knowledge graph, exposes enterprise-grade tool calling for data manipulation, and adjusts its behavior over time to match the user's recurring task patterns. After observing that a particular analyst consistently asks for charts when running churn analyses, the model should learn this association rapidly and reversibly — without retraining, without external orchestration, and without forgetting the underlying competencies acquired during pretraining.

Central claim. Three memory systems at three timescales — (a) symbolic graph retrieval, (b) parametric test-time-trained neural memory, and (c) Hebbian fast weights bolted onto LoRA — are complementary, not redundant, and can be coherently scheduled by a single neuromodulatory affect signal. This unification, not any individual component, is the architectural contribution.

Literature Review of Constituent Architectures

We survey each of the seven constituent strands, summarizing the canonical works, their core technical contribution, and their trade-offs when considered as candidates for inclusion in a unified small-model design.

2.1  Hybrid State-Space – Attention Architectures

The lineage of subquadratic sequence models begins with the structured state-space models of S4 9 and reaches its current canonical form in Mamba2 1, which reformulates the selective scan as a generalized matrix mixer admitting hardware-efficient parallel computation. Pure SSM stacks excel at long-context throughput but consistently underperform attention on tasks requiring precise associative recall — the MQAR benchmark of Arora et al. 10 made this gap quantitatively explicit. The natural resolution is hybridization: interleave SSM layers (for cheap context aggregation) with attention layers (for precision recall). Samba 3 demonstrated a 3:1 ratio of Mamba to sliding-window attention; Jamba 11 combined Mamba with full attention and MoE; Zamba2 12 used a shared attention block; Hymba 13 went further and ran Mamba and attention in parallel within the same layer. Across all four, the empirical finding is consistent: hybrids strictly dominate either parent at small scales.

Figure 1. Lineage of state-space models and their hybridization with attention. The proposed work sits in the hybrid family but extends it with a memory hierarchy (Section 4) absent in all predecessors.

Hybrid SSM + Attention — trade-offs
Pros
  • SSM layers scale linearly in sequence length; attention layers retain precise recall.
  • Empirically superior to either parent at the 100M–7B scale (Samba, Jamba, Hymba reports).
  • Inference latency at long context dominated by the cheaper SSM layers.
  • Training stable; well-precedented in published 1.5B–52B models.
 Cons
  • Adds two distinct kernel paths; engineering surface area larger than a pure transformer.
  • Optimal SSM:Attention ratio is task-dependent; usually rediscovered by ablation rather than principled choice.
  • External tooling (FlashAttention, etc.) targets pure attention — SSM kernels lag.

2.2  Differential Attention

Ye et al. 2 observed that standard softmax attention distributes a non-trivial fraction of its mass onto irrelevant context tokens, manifesting as “attention noise” that limits precise retrieval, complicates structured output, and contributes to hallucination. Their differential attention mechanism computes two attention maps with separate query/key projections and subtracts one from the other, with a learnable scalar controlling the cancellation strength. The result is sharper attention concentrations on the genuinely relevant tokens. The mechanism is a drop-in replacement for multi-head attention with negligible parameter overhead. Reported gains on long-context retrieval, in-context learning order-robustness, and hallucination detection are substantial; the technique has been adopted by several open-weight models released in late 2025.

Differential Attention — trade-offs
Pros
  • Drop-in upgrade over MHA; ~80 lines of code.
  • Empirically better on retrieval-heavy and structured-output tasks — exactly our precision-critical jobs.
  • Improves quantization robustness via reduced activation outliers.
 Cons
  • Still O(n²); does not address long-context cost (we rely on Mamba2 for that).
  • Slightly higher per-layer FLOPs (two QK matmuls instead of one).
  • Younger literature than vanilla MHA; long-tail edge cases less explored.

2.3  Phi-Style Small-Model Data Curation

The Phi series 14 demonstrated that a small model trained on aggressively curated, often synthetic, “textbook-quality” data can outperform much larger models trained on raw web text. The recipe inverts the prevailing Chinchilla-scaling logic: rather than scaling tokens with parameters, Phi held tokens roughly constant and dramatically raised per-token information density. We adopt this philosophy in our pretraining data mix (Section 6.2), tilting heavily toward FineWeb-Edu, Cosmopedia, OpenWebMath, and code corpora over raw CommonCrawl.

2.4  Enterprise Tool Calling: The Granite Lineage

IBM's Granite 3 series 6 produced one of the cleaner publicly documented tool-calling formats: a constrained JSON-schema block delimited by special tokens (<tool_call>{...}</tool_call>) with explicit response framing (<tool_response>...</tool_response>). The format trades expressivity for parseability and lends itself to formal grammar-constrained decoding. Combined with the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard 15 as a standardized eval, this region of the literature is now mature enough to be treated as a solved engineering problem — provided one has sufficient SFT data of comparable quality, which the Glaive and Hermes function-calling corpora supply under permissive licensing.

2.5  HippoRAG: Graph-Based Retrieval with Cognitive Scaffolding

HippoRAG 5 draws an explicit analogy to the role of the hippocampus in human episodic memory: rather than retrieving by isolated dense similarity, it extracts a knowledge graph from a document corpus (via OpenIE), seeds the graph with query-relevant nodes, and uses Personalized PageRank to propagate relevance across multi-hop paths before retrieving the resulting chunks. The technique is purely retrieval-time — no model training is required — and yields strong improvements on multi-hop QA benchmarks (MuSiQue, 2WikiMultiHopQA, HotpotQA). The successor work, From RAG to Memory 16, sharpens the graph-construction pipeline and adds an explicit memory-style update protocol.

Figure 2. The HippoRAG retrieval pattern. A user query identifies seed entities in the extracted knowledge graph; Personalized PageRank propagates relevance scores across multi-hop neighbors; chunks attached to high-scoring nodes are returned. Multi-hop questions that defeat naive dense retrieval (where no single chunk answers the query) succeed because the graph structure makes the inferential bridge explicit.

HippoRAG — trade-offs
Pros
  • Strong on multi-hop questions where naive dense RAG fails.
  • Graph is human-inspectable; failures are debuggable.
  • No model training required; pure retrieval-time mechanism.
  • Citations are first-class: every fact traces to a document chunk.
 Cons
  • Graph extraction quality is upstream-bottlenecked by the OpenIE model.
  • PPR computation latency scales with graph size; needs caching at deploy.
  • Cannot “feel” relevance the way a learned dense retriever can — misses purely semantic matches.
  • Knowledge updates require re-extraction of triples, not just re-embedding.

2.6  Titans: Test-Time-Trained Neural Memory

Behrouz et al.'s Titans 4 took the heretical step of allowing a small module's weights to update at inference time. The mechanism is structurally simple: a two-layer MLP is queried with each token's key vector, and the squared reconstruction error against the observed value vector defines a surprise signal. When surprise exceeds a threshold, a single SGD-style step updates the MLP's weights. Over a sequence, the MLP becomes a compressed associative memory of patterns the model has found surprising. Titans was introduced with three architectural variants — Memory-as-Context (MAC), Memory-as-Gating (MAG), and Memory-as-Layer (MAL) — differing in how the memory's output is combined with the backbone. MAG, in which memory and attention run in parallel and their outputs are combined via a learned sigmoid gate, is the most amenable to hybrid backbones because it integrates at every layer without extending the context length. Subsequent work 17 has reproduced and extended these findings.

Figure 3. The three Titans memory configurations. We adopt MAG because in a hybrid backbone where 75% of layers are Mamba2 (with no native query mechanism), parallel placement provides memory access at every layer without extending sequence length or serializing the stack.

Titans (MAG) — trade-offs
Pros
  • Memory genuinely “learns” during inference; not just a cache.
  • Fixed memory footprint (the MLP) regardless of how much data flows through.
  • Surprise-gated updates are biologically plausible and computationally cheap.
  • MAG variant requires no context extension — preserves Mamba2's long-context advantage.
 Cons
  • The youngest of our constituent ideas; long-term stability under heavy use unestablished.
  • Recall is lossy and uninterpretable (a 2-layer MLP is a black box).
  • Test-time gradient updates complicate deployment (autograd at inference, weight versioning).
  • Risk of memory poisoning by adversarial inputs unless surprise threshold is well-tuned.

2.7  Neuromodulation and Hebbian Plasticity

Biological brains do not learn with a single uniform signal; they modulate plasticity via neurochemicals (dopamine, serotonin, noradrenaline, cortisol) that gate when, where, and how strongly synapses change. HormoneT5 7 implemented an explicit hormone analogue in a T5 backbone, computing six scalar “hormone” values that modulate FFN gating. The Neuromodulation Gated Transformer 18 extended this with affect-gated layer outputs. Neither, however, applied the neuromodulatory signal to learning — both used it only for inference-time gating of activations.

Hebbian fast weights have a parallel lineage. Munkhdalai et al. 19 introduced fast-weight transformers; recent work on neuromodulated Hebbian plasticity in transformers 8 combined gradient-based meta-learning with Hebbian inner updates. AutoTool 20 built tool-transition graphs from trajectories, structurally similar to a co-firing matrix but populated by trajectory statistics rather than three-factor Hebbian rules.

We propose to merge these strands. A scalar affect signal (joy, frustration, confidence, fatigue, boredom) modulates not activations but learning rates across all writable memory tiers. A Hebbian co-firing matrix, populated by reward-gated three-factor rules, biases tool-selection at inference. The unification — a single neuromodulatory state governing parametric weights (Titans), explicit fast weights (Hebbian H), and low-rank deltas (LoRA) simultaneously — appears to be original.

Hormone + Hebbian neuromodulation — trade-offs
Pros
  • Provides a principled scheduler for when to update each memory tier.
  • Inspectable: the Hebbian H matrix can literally be printed and read.
  • The “step back” behavior (frustration ⇒ rollback + exploration) emerges naturally.
  • Aligns with three-factor learning rules well-established in computational neuroscience.
 Cons
  • Reward signal design is brittle; spurious joy can reinforce wrong behaviors.
  • No public dataset has paired correction·retry·satisfaction signals — cold-start problem.
  • The Hebbian H biases tool-selection — could produce overconfident, “rut” behavior if decay is too slow.
  • Adds five new hyperparameters per memory tier; ablation surface is large.

2.8  Online LoRA and Continual Learning

The body of work on continual learning in language models 21 has converged on a small number of stable techniques: low-rank adapters (LoRA) 22 isolate update capacity from the frozen base; experience replay buffers preserve representative samples of past distributions; snapshot/rollback mechanisms allow recovery from degradation. Online-LoRA 23 demonstrated that adapter updates can occur during deployment, provided the adapter rank is small and the base remains frozen. The TRACE benchmark 24 provides a standard evaluation harness for measuring backward transfer (forgetting) and forward transfer (positive interference) across sequential tasks.

The unsolved residual challenges — reward hacking, distribution drift, catastrophic forgetting under uncontrolled signals — are precisely what the neuromodulatory scheduler is intended to address. The hormones gate when the LoRA updates and the snapshot ring permits recovery after degradation.

Proposed Architecture: The Frankenstein

3.1  Overall shape and parameter budget

The backbone is a 24-layer hybrid stack with a 3:1 ratio of Mamba2 to differential-attention blocks, following the Samba pattern with differential attention substituted for vanilla MHA. Attention layers are placed at positions 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, and 24 — serving as precision checkpoints distributed evenly through the stack and at the output. Table 1 summarizes hyperparameters.

The Tri-Memory Neuromodulation System

This section details the mechanism that constitutes the paper's central contribution.

4.1  Tier 1: HippoRAG external graph memory

Documents are ingested through a Phi-3-mini-based OpenIE pipeline that extracts (subject, predicate, object) triples. Triples are aggregated into a NetworkX graph, with document-chunk pointers attached to nodes for citation. At query time, embeddings of named entities in the query seed Personalized PageRank; chunks attached to high-scoring nodes are retrieved. Retrieved chunks are injected at the 6 differential-attention layers (and only those) via a cross-attention adapter whose Q comes from the layer's hidden state and whose K/V come from chunk embeddings. The Mamba2 layers see only the original token stream.

4.2  Tier 2: Titans MAG neural memory

A single 2-layer MLP (d_in=1536, d_hidden=2048, d_out=1536, ~15M parameters) is shared across all 24 backbone layers, with the layer index added to the key as a small positional bias. At every layer, the backbone's block output (whether Mamba2 or DiffAttn) and the MLP's output are combined via a learned sigmoid gate. The MLP's weights update at test time: when surprise (the squared reconstruction error against the observed value) exceeds a threshold τsurprise, a single Adam step is taken with learning rate ηtitans. Critically, both τsurprise and ηtitans are modulated by the hormone scalars (Section 4.5).

4.3  Tier 3: Runtime LoRA with snapshot ring

Rank-16 LoRA adapters are attached to the Q/K/V/O projections of every differential-attention layer and to the input/output projections of every Mamba2 block, totaling ~30M trainable parameters. The base model is frozen. Two distinct update channels exist:

  • Online updates occur during deployment, triggered by surprise and gated by hormone signals (only when joy is high, frustration low, and the interaction was user-confirmed). Single-step Adam with hormone-modulated LR.
  • Sleep training occurs nightly (or when fatigue exceeds a threshold), running a full epoch over the day's buffered interactions with a standard cosine schedule, followed by a snapshot.

A ring buffer holds the last 8 LoRA snapshots. Every 100 online updates, a canary eval (100 frozen prompts covering tool-call format, RAG faithfulness, and reasoning) runs; any metric drop >5% triggers automatic rollback to the most recent snapshot with acceptable metrics.

4.4  Tier 0b: Hebbian co-firing matrix H

This is the fast pre-LoRA learning short-circuit. We track features that co-fire in positively-reinforced interactions:

Features tracked per turn (sparse indicators):
  - task_intent  (k-means cluster id over query embedding, k=512)
  - tools_used   (~64 distinct tool types)
  - retrieval_sources (k=256 HippoRAG community ids)
  - response_shape (table / chart / prose / code, ~16 ids)

H is a sparse |F|×|F| matrix with |F| ≈ 850.

Three-factor Hebbian update (eligibility × reward × modulator):
  if joy_t > τ_joy:
    for each active pair (i,j) in turn t:
        H[i,j] += η_H · joy_t · co_act(i,j)
  if frustration_t > τ_frust:
        H[i,j] *= (1 − decay · frustration_t)

At inference, H biases tool-selection logits:
  logits_tool  +=  λ · H[task_intent_id, :] @ tool_embeddings

The matrix is small (<100 MB), interpretable (one can print(H[churn_intent]) and read which tools and response shapes the system associates with churn analysis), and instantly reversible (zeroing an entry undoes a learned association). After a few user-confirmed “churn analysis → chart” interactions, H[churn_id, make_chart_id] rises; the fifth churn query receives a tool-selection bias toward make_chart before any LoRA delta has propagated through the slower Tier 3 channel.

4.5  Tier 0a: Hormone scalars and unified modulation

Five EMA-tracked scalars define the model's affective state at any moment:

ScalarRaised byEffect when high
joyUser-accepted outputs, successful tool calls, low retry rateRaise LoRA LR on recent traces; reinforce co-firing in H
frustrationUser corrections, tool failures, repeated bad outputsCut LoRA LR; raise sampling temperature; consult HippoRAG more eagerly; if > τpanic, rollback last snapshot
confidenceLow prediction entropy combined with good outcomesSkip retrieval to reduce latency; lower sampling temperature
fatigueTime since last snapshot; number of online updates accumulatedForce snapshot; schedule sleep-training run
boredomSustained low variance in Titans surprise signalLower τsurprise so Titans updates more eagerly

Figure 5. The neuromodulatory feedback loop. Each turn produces a reward signal (joy/frustration) extracted from user reaction. The hormone EMA state updates accordingly. On the next turn, hormones modulate the learning rates and update thresholds of all writable memory tiers, producing a closed loop in which the model's mood literally controls its own plasticity.

Inference-Time Data Flow

One complete inference turn, end to end:

1.  User query arrives
2.  Embedding model classifies task_intent (cluster id over k=512 clusters)
3.  HippoRAG: PPR over knowledge graph, top-k chunks retrieved
4.  Hebbian H biases the tool-selection prior:
       logits_tool += λ · H[task_intent, :] @ tool_embeddings
5.  Forward pass through 24 hybrid layers:
       for each layer L:
           backbone_out = Mamba2(x) or DiffAttn(x)
           memory_out   = TitansMLP(x, layer_id=L)
           gate         = σ(W_gate · x)
           x_next       = backbone_out + gate ⊙ memory_out
       at the 6 DiffAttn layers (4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24):
           x_next = x_next + cross_attn(x_next, retrieved_chunks)
6.  Sampling temperature = base_T · (1 + α·frustration − β·confidence)
7.  Generate response (possibly invoking tools)
8.  Tool execution; observe outputs and user reaction
9.  Hormone scalars updated (EMA over recent outcomes)
10. If Titans surprise > τ_surprise(boredom): MLP gradient step
11. If joy > τ_joy AND interaction confirmed-positive:
       Hebbian H update; LoRA online step with LR modulated by joy
12. Every 100 online updates: canary eval; rollback if regressed
13. Interaction buffered for nightly sleep-training

7.2  Paper-original custom probes

Four probes do not exist in the literature and must be invented:

  1. MAG memory-attention probe. A needle is planted in the Titans memory state via a surprise-triggered update (not via context). The probe queries the model k forward passes later and measures recall.
  2. Hormone gating eval. Stability-plasticity probe: alternate a stable task with a novel task; measure forgetting on the stable task while learning the novel one. Compare hormones-on vs hormones-off.
  3. Joint retrieval-writeback eval. Stream new documents into HippoRAG; query the implied facts; measure how knowledge-graph mutation under load affects downstream QA accuracy.
  4. Online-LoRA tool-schema retention. A composition of BFCL and TRACE: do continual updates on miscellaneous tasks corrupt the model's tool-calling competence acquired in Stage 4 SFT?

Discussion and Publishability

9.1  Where the novelty actually sits

None of the individual blocks in this design is new. Mamba2 hybrids are well-studied; Titans MAG is a year old at submission; HippoRAG is a year old; differential attention is six months old; Granite tool calling is a known training recipe; Hebbian fast weights have a forty-year lineage; LoRA snapshot rollback is engineering folklore by now. Combining these is also not, in itself, new — many groups stitch together components.

What appears to be original, after our literature audit, is the specific claim that three independent memory systems operating at three distinct timescales can be coherently scheduled by a single neuromodulatory affect signal. The closest prior work modulates activations (HormoneT5, NMG-Transformer) or modulates a single learning system (online LoRA, Hebbian transformers). No published work uses one affect signal to gate writes simultaneously across symbolic graph memory, test-time-trained neural memory, fast-weight Hebbian memory, and slow-weight LoRA memory. This is the publishable thesis.

9.2  Venue and audience

The most natural venue is the Conference on Language Modeling (COLM) 2026, whose scope explicitly includes small-model methods, memory, and training procedures. NeurIPS 2026 main track is plausible if the ablation set is unusually strong. ICLR 2027 works if framed architecturally. The Workshops on Babylm and Memory in NLP at EMNLP serve as fallbacks if main-track venues reject on integration-paper grounds.

9.3  Honest assessment of risk to the thesis

Three weaknesses a reviewer will probe:

  1. “You can't prove the hormones are doing anything load-bearing.” Mitigated by the 2³ modulator factorial; we expect at least the Hebbian on/off and rollback on/off axes to show clear effects.
  2. “Three memory systems is two too many.” Mitigated by the five-condition memory factorial; we expect each tier to contribute on a distinct benchmark (HippoRAG on multi-hop QA, Titans on BABILong, Hebbian on TRACE-style continual eval).
  3. “A 700M model is too small for these effects to be visible.” The honest response is that this is a learning-vehicle paper as much as a contribution paper. We acknowledge scale as a limitation and present the model as a proof of architectural concept, not a frontier system.
